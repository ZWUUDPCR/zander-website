abstract: Is the effect of climate anomalies on communal violence conditional on the level of gender inequality? This thesis investigates whether the impact of climate anomalies on communal violence is moderated by gender inequality. Drawing on subnational panel data from 1990 to 2022 and employing large-N statistical models, this study assesses the relationship using the six-month Standardised Precipitation–Evapotranspiration Index (SPEI) to capture climate anomalies and the Subnational Gender Development Index (SGDI) to operationalise gender inequality. The empirical strategy combines Firth’s logistic regression and zero-inflated negative binomial models to examine how the likelihood, frequency, and intensity of communal violence vary across different levels of gender inequality. While the results reveal no consistent direct association between climate anomalies and communal violence, they demonstrate that gender inequality conditions this relationship: under drought conditions, higher levels of gender inequality are linked to increased likelihood and intensity of violence. These findings underscore the importance of integrating gender as a moderating variable in climate–conflict research and offer a methodological contribution to incorporating gendered mechanisms within large-N empirical frameworks.
